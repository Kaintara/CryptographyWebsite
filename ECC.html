Elliptic Curve Cryptography

Elliptic Curves are used to create digital signatures that ensure authenticity in the ownership of cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin. This is due to a special property of Elliptic Curves that not only creates a harder version of the discrete logarithm problem but also generates significantly smaller private and public keys in practice.

How does Elliptic curve cryptography work?

Elliptic curves have a special property which allows two points to be ‘added’ to get another point on the curve. Any line that goes through an elliptic curve can only have at most three points of intersection so to add two points, a line must be drawn that intersects those two points and another point on the elliptic curve. Reflect this point over the x-axis and that is the addition of those two points.

For Elliptic curve cryptography a single point is multiplied by adding itself multiple times. To do this, a tangent is drawn to the curve at the chosen point. The next point of intersection on the curve with that tangent is reflected across the x-axis. The chosen point is called the Generator Point. The private key is the random number picked to multiply the generator point to generate a public key. Therefore, when a known elliptic curve is used over a finite field P = dG where P is the public key, d is the private key and G is the generator point.
